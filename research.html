The Impact of Merit-Based Scholarships on Student Outcomes: Evidence from Ontario

Revise & resubmit at Economics of Education Review

Abstract

Merit-based scholarships are widely used by post-secondary institutions to attract high-achieving students, yet causal evidence on their effectiveness remains mixed. This paper employs a fuzzy regression discontinuity design that exploits undisclosed high school grade cutoffs to evaluate a lump-sum merit-based entrance scholarship at the University of Toronto for domestic undergraduates. The estimates indicate a null average effect on yield that masks pronounced heterogeneity: the scholarship substantially raises enrolment among students from lower-income neighbourhoods, from outside the Greater Toronto Area, and those who have a stronger prior preference for the University. Descriptive estimates for first-year GPA and graduation are null, which suggests that the scholarship does not substantially improve the academic quality of enrollees.

Persevere or Pivot? The Causal Impact of Grade Shocks on the College Major Decision

with Annabel Thornton · submitted

Abstract

Using variation in exam scheduling, we estimate the causal effect of grade shocks on college major choice. Writing two exams in quick succession reduces performance on the latter by 1.05 percentage points and subsequently reduces the likelihood of majoring and graduating in that subject area by 6% and 9%, respectively. We find that a 1 percentage point drop in course grade raises the probability of graduating in a lower-earning subject area by 24%. These findings underscore the sensitivity of long-term human capital decisions to relatively minor academic disruptions.
